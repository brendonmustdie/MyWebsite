Geography of Information

Re: Towards a study of information geographies: (im)mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information.

I’ll say it, just in case no one else will: this paper is deeply inaccesible. Undefined (or badly defined) terms are strung together in long sentences that are hard to make sense of. I’m trying my best to work through it but holy hell, is it hard to grasp ANY information being shared. (Which is ironic, considering it’s about access to information.)

This paper explores geographic distributions of information - participation and representation. It uses existing statistics and previously unmapped data to provide a “broad survey of a rang of key platforms”. The paper seeks to demonstate that in addition to uneven geographical access, there is also uneven participation and representation.

Now here’s an interesting concept: the paper poses that Information has always been mobile, and it eventually developed the need to become immutable (unchanging) and consistent, because power and information become connected. This is an incredibly interesting concept - especially in thinking about how information (and misinformation) on the internet can be changed and spread.

The concept that physical places are not just physical places, but concepts that are layered in reviews, stories, pictures, maps, and postcards is also interesting. The paper states that the combination of these “layers” of each object influence how it is thought about and interacted with, and therefore changes the place itself. Which means that geographic representation is important. “Places invisible or discounted in representations are equally invisble in practice to many people. A restuarant omitted from a map can cease to be a restaurant if nobody finds it.”

The access to information has been drastically more widespread in the past decades - as we experience a revolution that makes computers and internet more and more accessible. In theory this should break down the barriers between people and information but it doesn’t, necessarily.

Even internet cafes cost money, that some people don’t have. Academic papers lie behind paywalls that you can’t access unless you’re from an institution or willing to pay for it - again, this wouldn’t be such an issue if it was just one website. If Academia.edu had every paper on it in the world, most people wouldn’t mind paying for a subscription. But it doesn’t. So here we are.

This widespread information does have some positive effects, though. Tapscott and Williams (2006) and Shirky (2011) argue that digital participation allows civillians to play a bigger role in politics and the content that affects their lives.

There is an echoed sentiment that once the internet is widespread, all people in the world will have equal access to information and opportunities. In this vein, Wikipedia seeks to ‘contain the sum of all human knowledge’; and Google’s “core mission” is to organize information, making it accessible and useful. (Google 2015)

The paper includes a figure using the data of 2011 study by the Wold Bank, tracking internet users and connections per country since the 90s - it’s calculated using the internet penetraion of each country and is displayed in a cartogram in which the size of each country is determined by it’s population of internet users (each hexagon presenting a third of a million internet users).

The paper uses this study to draw conclusions about different regions but the bottom line comes to: “- most people on our planet remain without access.”

The paper also touches on affordability, the study the quote talks, not only about the cost, but also the cost relative to the average income of people in that country, which they use to explain the data in the previous study. The figure is published by the International Telecommunication Union (ITU 2013) in 2013.

“Without the ability and means to connect, any chance to participate in or contribure to representations of their localities is simply impossible.”

This paper adequetly demonstrates that there are uneven geographies of access to communication, and uneven geographies of participation (and therefore representation) are sometimes being amplified rather than alleviated. It’s very well researched, and demonstrates clearly that even the spread of the internet has not donw much to alleviate lack of access to informaiton in the global South. I really enjoy how this paper points out the effects of colonialism - but I do wish it was more recent. I’m sure the studies would reveal different (thankfully) information now than it did then.

References:
